A list of complicated words, Egyptological terms and historical terms that are used and defined in the site.

Abu Simbel
A temple built by Ramesses II that is located south of Aswan.

Abydos
A town in Upper Egypt. The ancient Egyptians believed that the head of the god Osiris had been buried at Abydos. For this reason, Abydos was always an important religious site.

Adze
A tool used in woodworking. An adze had a short metal blade attached at an angle to a wooden handle.

Afterlife
The Egyptians believed that, when an ordinary person died, they passed through the underworld where they would be judged. If they they were judged to be pure and good, they would go to a place that was very much like Egypt, and live there forever. The Egyptians believed that when a pharaoh died, he went to live with his 'father' the sun god Amun-Ra.

Amulet/s
A small trinket or charm that was meant to protect the owner from evil spells or actions.

Apis Bull
A sacred animal to the ancient Egyptians whose cult was centred around Memphis in Lower Egypt. It was very rare for a black bull to be born with white markings, so when one was, the Egyptians saw it as a sacred sign and worshipped the animal. One bull at a time with the white markings was named the 'Apis Bull'. The Apis Bull was honoured in ceremonies and then mummified and buried in a tomb when it died. The mother of the Apis Bull was called the 'Isis Cow' and was also worshipped.

Appointed
To be named to a post by the pharaoh or an important official.

Archaeological
Something which relates to archaeology. Archaeology is the study of past cultures based on artifacts, texts and monuments left behind by that culture.

Archaeologist
A person who studies past cultures through studying the artifacts, texts and monuments left behind by that culture. Archaeologists gain much of their information through excavating (digging) at the sites of ancient settlements.

Archer
A person who shoots a bow and arrow.

Architect
A person who designs buildings.

Ascending
Moving upward; going up.

Asiatic
A term sometimes used to describe people who came from the ancient Levant (an old term used to describe the area of the Middle East covering the modern states of: Israel, Jordan, Lebanon and Syria).

Aswan High Dam
A dam built in the 1960s to control the annual flooding of the Nile, and to create a reservoir. The water flowing from the Nile sources collects south of the dam forming Lake Nasser which covers over with water much of the area that was ancient Nubia.

Banquet
A large dinner party; a feast.

Barge
A large boat, also called a 'bark' or 'barque'.

Bark
A boat (also spelled 'barque').

Barren
A place where nothing grows.

Book of the Dead
A collection of about 200 spells written on papyrus scrolls, which would have been placed inside the coffin with the mummy. These spells were supposed to help a person pass safely through the underworld.

Bronze
A metal made from mixing copper and tin.

Cairo
The capital of modern Egypt, located in northern Egypt.

Canopic Jar
Jars used to hold internal organs. The lids of Canopic jars represented four gods called the "four sons of Horus".

Cartouche
The oval-shaped symbol in which the names of kings and queens of Egypt were written.

Cast
A model of an object that is made from pouring plaster or metal into a mould.

Cataract
Rocky areas in the river bed of the Nile which make the water flow unevenly (rapids). There are six 'cataracts' on the Nile between Aswan and Khartoum.

Causeway
A paved pathway.

Cedar
A strong, reddish-brown wood that was valuable in ancient Egypt. The ancient Egyptians used cedar to make trading boats and furniture. However, this important wood did not grow in ancient Egypt, and it had to be imported from Phoenicia (modern Lebanon).

Cemetery
A place where people are buried.

Chaos
The state of being without order or organization.

Chisel
A straight tool used to carve stone and wood. Chisels were usually used with a mallet or stone.

Chronology
A sequence of events in the past; a way of organising past events by dates or historical knowledge.

Coarse
Rough; something of lesser quality.

Cobra
A deadly snake that used to be common in Egypt. Before it strikes, it raises up its head and shows its fangs. It was the symbol of Wadjyt, the goddess of Lower Egypt.

Complex
1. A group of buildings that are connected in some way; 2. Complicated.

Composite
Something that is made from a combination of materials.

Constellation
A group of stars.

Contours
The outline of a natural feature of the landscape such as a mountain or a coastline.

Controversial
Something or someone that is disputed or out of the ordinary.

Copper
An orange-brown coloured metal.

Cosmetics
Makeup and/or lotions used by people to make them look beautiful.

Dahshur
An area on the west bank of the Nile where the ancient Egyptians built pyramids. It is located south of modern Cairo.

Deceased
A person who has died.

Decipher / Deciphering
To work out the meaning of something that is difficult to understand.

Decompose
To rot.

Dedicated
To name a temple for a god or goddess, and honour that god or goddess there.

Dehydrated
To dry out.

Dendera
A town in Upper Egypt.

Descending
Moving down; going down.

Disassembled
To be taken apart.

Ebony
A strong, dark wood. Ebony was very valuable to the ancient Egyptians, who used it to make fine furniture. Ebony grew south of Egypt and was acquired through tribute and trade.

Edfu
A town in middle Egypt.

Egyptologist
A person who studies ancient Egypt. This term was used a lot in the 19th century to describe people who were very interested in ancient Egypt. Today, 'Egyptologist' is most often used to describe people who go to university to learn about ancient Egypt.

Electrum
A metal that is a mixture of gold and silver.

Embalmer
A person who treats dead bodies with preservatives to keep them looking lifelike. Embalmers in ancient Egypt used natron and oils in embalming.

Embalming
To treat a dead body with preservatives to keep it looking lifelike. Ancient Egyptian embalmers used natron and oils to preserve bodies.

Emphasises
To attach an extra degree of importance to a statement, idea or detail in order to highlight it.

Enriched
To add value or quality to something.

Eternity
Forever; endless time

Exotic
Something that is unusual or mysterious - often from a foreign land.

Facade
The front of a building.

Ferocious
Something or someone who is fierce.

Flint
A hard stone, used to make arrowheads and stone tools.

Funerary
Something connected with a funeral or burial.

Furnace
A large, hot oven used for heating metal and other materials to high temperatures.

Giza
A town located near Cairo in northern Egypt. The site of three large stone pyramids built during the fourth dynasty.

Glazed Composition
Glazed composition (also known as faience) was a popular material used for making beads and small statues. The term is used to describe objects that were made of a sandy or clay core, covered with a glaze (usually blue) and fired.

Harmony
When something is well-balanced and working well.

Hide
The skin of an animal, including the fur.

Hieroglyphic/ Hieroglyphs
Hieroglyphic writing was a form of writing used by the ancient Egyptians. The individual characters are called ‘hieroglyphs'

Hittites
A powerful empire that was based in the area that is now Turkey. The Hittites were powerful in about 1300-1200 B.C., and struggled with the Egyptians for control in the Levant (an old term used to describe the area of the Middle East covering the modern states of: Israel, Jordan, Lebanon and Syria).

Hyksos
The Hyksos were a group of people from the Levant, who settled in Egypt at the end of the Middle Kingdom. They brought new technologies with them to Egypt, such as the chariot.

Hypostyle
A hall with many columns supporting a roof.

Imported
Something which has been grown (or made) in one country, but is brought into another country and sold there. For instance, the Egyptians did not have much timber in their country, so they imported it from places where there were tall trees.

Incense
Something that produces a sweet smell when it is burned.

Inlay
A material like ivory or stone that is inserted into, or applied to, the surface of another material as decoration.

Innundation
The yearly flooding of the Nile River.

Irrigation
A system involving canals that was used to deliver water to fields.

Ivory
The name given to the material made from the tusks of an elephant or teeth of a hippopotamus. The ancient Egyptians used ivory to make small objects and to decorate furniture.

Kilt
A type of skirt worn by men in ancient Egypt.

Kohl
A powder used as eye makeup in ancient Egypt.

Lake Nasser
The reservoir that was formed after the Nile was blocked by the Aswan High Dam. Lake Nasser covers much of the area that was ancient Nubia.

Levant
The name used to describe an area of the Middle East covering the modern states of: Israel, Jordan, Lebanon and Syria.

Limestone
A common, fine-grained stone.

Linen
A woven fabric made of flax (plant) fibres.

Lintel
The block of stone or wood that is located above the door in a doorway.

Lotus
A plant also known as a 'water lily' that lives in the water. The flower emerges from the water to bloom in the air. The Egyptians saw the lotus flower as a symbol of rebirth. They believed that at the beginning of time the young sun-god had come out from the water.

Mace
The mace was a weapon shaped like a club that was commonly used in ancient Egypt from the Predynastic period.

Macedonia
A country north of Greece.

Mastaba/s
An Arabic word meaning 'bench' that is commonly used to describe an early ancient Egyptian tomb. Mastabas have an underground burial chamber and an above ground offering chapel. These chapels look like the benches found outside some modern Egyptian homes, thus the word 'mastaba' is used to describe them.

Mesopotamia
An area which is now within modern Iraq.

Middle Kingdom
A period of ancient Egyptian history that lasted from about 2055 B.C. to 1650 B.C..

Mortuary
Something connected with death.

Mould
A mould is made from pressing an object into wet plaster or soft wax so that the object's shape, texture, and decorations make an impression into the plaster or wax. A copy (cast) of the object can then be made by pouring a material that will harden (like plaster) into the mould.

Mummify / Mummifying / Mummification
To preserve a dead body by embalming and wrapping it with cloth.

Natron
A natural salt used to dry out dead bodies and internal organs in the mummification process.

Natural Resources
Something that is easily available to the people who live in an area, rather than something that needs to be brought into the area from another place. For instance, tall trees were not one of Egypt's natural resources, so they needed to import trees from a country where there were many forests, like Phoenicia (modern-day Lebanon).

Near East
A vague name used to describe the countries to the northeast of the Egyptian border. This area includes the Levant and land directly to the east of it.The Levant is the name used to describe an area of the Middle East covering the modern states of: Israel, Jordan, Lebanon and Syria.

New Kingdom
A period of ancient Egyptian history that lasted from about 1550 B.C. to 1069 B.C..

Nobleman
The upper class; people with wealth and high social position.

Nubia
A term used to refer to the land that lay directly south of ancient Egypt. Today the northern part of ancient Nubia lies within the borders of modern Egypt, and the southern part within the borders of modern Sudan.

Nubian
A person from Nubia.Nubia is the term used to refer to the land that lay directly south of ancient Egypt. Today the northern part of ancient Nubia lies within the borders of modern Egypt, and the southern part within the borders of modern Sudan.

Obelisk
A tall, thin, four-sided monument with a pyramid shape at the very top.

Oblong
A shape that looks like a flattened circle.

Offering
Something that is offered to the gods. Offerings could be food, valuables, special oils, or animals to be sacrificed.

Old Kingdom
A period of ancient Egyptian history that lasted from about 2686 B.C. to 2181 B.C..

Papyrus
Papyrus was a reed that grew in ancient Egypt that was used in many different ways. It was cut into strips, woven together, pounded, and dried to make sheets which were written on. Papyrus was also gathered, tied together in bunches, and used to make small fishing boats. 'Papyri' is the plural form of the word papyrus.

Pharaoh
Pharaoh' is the term we use today to describe the rulers of ancient Egypt. 'Pharaoh' is actually a Greek word that is based on an Egyptian word that meant 'great house'. When this word was first used, it referred to the palace and its greatness, not just to the ruler himself. However, later in Egyptian history the title 'pharaoh' meant ruler.

Philae
A town in southern Egypt.

Phoenicia
The Greek name for the country that existed in the first and second millenia B.C. located where Lebanon is today. The Phoenicians are known today as the inventors of the alphabet, and as excellent shipbuilders, sailors, and navigators.

Plateau

An area of land with a flat surface.

Polar
Something related to the poles of the Earth.

Precious
Something that is very special or valuable.

Primeval
Something related to the beginning of the world.

Processions
A formal parade of people and/or animals.

Profession
A job that involves some kind of learning or training.

Prosperity
Success and/or wealth

Quarry / Quarries
A place where rock is cut out from the ground.

Recede / Receding
To pull back; to withdraw

Reconstruct
To rebuild something; to put something back together.

Reed
A plant with a straight, tall stalk that grows in a marshy place.

Regalia
The ceremonial dress and objects that were carried by the pharaoh to show that he was the ruler.

Reign
The period during which a king or queen (or pharaoh) rules a country.

Relief
The word used to describe scenes, symbols and figures that are carved onto a wall or slab of rock. Sunken relief is when the main aspects of the picture are carved into the rock (or other hard surface). Raised relief is when the background of the scene is carved out so that the main figures and symbols stick out from the wall.

Resin
A sticky, thick fluid produced by certain trees and plants.

Respect
To honour or hold in high esteem.

Resurrection
Rebirth.

Ritual
A religious ceremony.

Rosetta (Rashid)
A town in the Nile Delta.

Sacred
Something that is connected with a god or goddess, or something that has general religious importance; holy.

Saqqara
An area on the west bank of the Nile where the ancient Egyptians built pyramids. It is located south of modern Cairo.

Sarcophagus
A large stone or wood box in which coffins were placed for burial.

Satellite
Something that is connected with, but not attached to, something larger.

Scarab
1. A type of beetle, also called a dung beetle. The scarab beetle was a symbol of rebirth for the ancient Egyptians. 2. An amulet or object that was shaped like a scarab beetle.

Sceptre
The symbol of kingship that would be carried by the pharaoh.

Scholar
A person who is well-educated.

Scribe
An ancient Egyptian man who learned to read and write hieroglyphs and hieratic (a quicker and shorter form of hieroglyphs). Scribes had two main duties, first, they were the only people in ancient Egypt who could read and write the sacred hieroglyphs that would appear on temples and in tombs, and second, they kept government records and wrote letters for the pharaoh.

Script
A group of signs and letters used to make words.

Scroll
A rolled up sheet (or sheets) of papyrus.

Silt
Small particles of sand or earth that are carried in moving water.

Sinai
A peninsula located east of Egypt.

Sinew
The tendons (muscles) of an animal.

Slope
The angle of the side of a building or mountain.

Sphinx
An imaginary animal with the body of a lion and the head of a person (or sometimes a falcon).

Spiritually
Related to the spiritual nature of something.

Staff
A long stick made of either wood or metal that is carried by a person to show their position in the government or the priesthood.

Stelai/stele
Stone slabs that would stand upright in a temple or important place. Important information was recorded on stelae. The singular of stelai is stele.

Succeed
To become the next ruler. For instance, Ramesses II succeeded his father as pharaoh.

Suez Canal
A canal that joins the Red Sea and the Mediterranean Sea.Building the canal made it easier to transport goods from the east to the Mediterranean; thus creating an important trade route.The canal was started in 1859, and was completed in 1869. It was originally 200 feet (60.1 metres) wide, and 24 feet (7.3 metres) deep. The canal has been made wider and deeper over the years, and now it is 500 feet (152.4 metres) wide and about 33 feet (10.1 metres) deep.

Suspicious
Wary; to be unsure of someone or something because you think that they might try to do you some harm.

Symbol
An image which represents an idea or belief.

Symbolic
An image which is used to communicate an idea or belief.

Taxes
A percentage of a person's yearly income or crops that was collected by the pharaoh.

Thebes
The ancient Greek name for the town which is now called Luxor. Luxor is located in the southern part of Egypt. There are many important archaeological sites in Luxor, including the Valley of the Kings, the temples of Luxor and Karnak, and Deir el-Bhari (the mortuary temple of Hatshepsut).

Trade
The exchange of goods for money, or for goods of equal value.

Tribute
Raw materials (like gold), or things that were made (like gold bracelets), that were given as a 'gift' by a country that was under the control of another country to the ruler of the controlling country.

Tunic
A type of long shirt.

Underworld
The place where a dead person's soul would go to be judged. The ancient Egyptians believed that a person's soul would have to pass a series of tests in the underworld before they were allowed to enter the afterlife where they would live forever.

Unified
To be brought together; to become one.

Uraeus
Uraeus is the Greek word used to describe the cobra that appears in Egyptian art (for instance on the pharaoh's clothing or headdress).

Valley of the Kings
An area on the west bank of the Nile near modern Luxor. The Valley of the Kings was where the pharaohs of the New Kingdom (about 1550 B.C. to 1069 B.C.) were buried in rock-cut tombs.

Ventilation
The flow of air between an enclosed area and the outside

Vizier
A high official in the government of ancient Egypt who was an advisor to the pharaoh.
